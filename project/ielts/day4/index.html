雅思口语语料库 - Day 4

雅思口语语料库 — C20 Test 4 Speaking（Personal Qualities / News / Discussion）

一、Part 1: Personal Qualities

Q1: What do you think your best personal qualities are?

> “Honestly, I’d say it’s self-reflection. I truly believe that looking inward and asking yourself what you could’ve done better is the only way to actually level up as a person. I do this pretty regularly — after a presentation, after a tough week — and I think it’s the main reason I’ve been able to keep progressing.”

Q2: Do you have the same personal qualities as your parents?

> “Honestly, I’d say we’re all pretty meticulous about things. My parents drilled into me from a young age that you need to pay attention to the details — doesn’t matter if it’s household stuff or study problems. And they didn’t just talk about it; they actually practiced what they preached. I grew up watching them handle everything carefully, so that habit just rubbed off on me.”

Q3: What personal qualities are important to you in a friend?

> “I’d say sincerity and kindness are the most important. Everyone has flaws, and you’re bound to have disagreements with friends. But as long as I feel their core nature is genuinely good and honest, I can look past the smaller issues. Those don’t really matter in the long run.”

Q4: Do you think you have the personal qualities to be a good/successful leader?

> “To be honest, I don’t think so — not yet, anyway. I feel like I’m still missing some key qualities. But it’s definitely something I aspire to, so I’m constantly trying to learn and improve myself.”

二、Part 2: Describe a time when you had a long discussion about a news story

IG夺冠（英雄联盟全球总决赛）

> “Well, I’d like to talk about the time when IG won the League of Legends World Championship. I was in high school at the time, and my classmates and I talked about it for days. > > Basically, the moment they won, every guy in my class came in the next day asking, ‘Did you watch the game? IG is insane!’ For all of us who played LOL, it was huge because it was the first time a Chinese team had won the world title. We’d been following the tournament for years and had faced so many disappointments before, so this finally felt like the world was hearing our story. > > Even our teacher brought it up in class, which was pretty rare. We just couldn’t stop talking about it because it was this historic moment that we’d all been waiting for for so long.”

三、Part 3: News / Discussion programmes

Q1: How do most people find out about the news in your country?

> “I would contend that digital platforms, particularly mobile devices, have become the predominant conduit for news consumption across all demographics, encompassing both the elderly and the younger generation. The ubiquity of smartphones has effectively supplanted traditional print and broadcast media as the primary source of information.”

Q2: Are people more interested in local news than national news?

> “Honestly, I’d say people care about both equally. Local news is obviously relevant because it’s about what’s happening right around you — your neighbourhood, your city, your commute. But national news isn’t something people tune out either, because things like economic policy or education reform directly shape your future too. Most people I know follow both — they just consume them differently.”

Q3: How important is it to know about international news?

> “Well, for the average person, I’d say it’s not absolutely essential. But if you’re working in an international field — like business, tech, or academia — or if you aspire to contribute on a global scale, then keeping up with international news is pretty important. It really depends on your personal and professional goals.”

Q4: Why are discussion programmes involving members of the public popular on TV and radio?

> “Honestly, I think it’s because they feel real. When a random person calls in or sits on a panel and talks about their actual life problems or opinions, the audience is like, ‘That’s exactly how I feel.’ It creates this instant connection. Plus, there’s a sense that anyone’s voice matters, which makes people feel invested in the show.”

Q5: What kinds of people want to take part in discussion programmes?

> “Well, I’d say there are a few types. Some people are just naturally opinionated — they love sharing their views on public platforms. Others might have gone through specific experiences — like consumer disputes or neighbourhood conflicts — and they want to get their story heard or seek advice. And then there are people who simply enjoy being on camera or crave a bit of attention.”

Q6: Do discussion programmes influence people in a good or bad way?

> “I would argue that the influence is ambivalent and context-dependent. Constructive programmes can elevate public discourse, foster empathy, and broaden perspectives by exposing audiences to diverse lived experiences. Conversely, programmes that prioritise sensationalism — deliberately amplifying discord or selecting participants for maximal controversy — may exacerbate social polarisation and degrade the quality of public debate. The net effect is therefore determined by editorial intent.”
