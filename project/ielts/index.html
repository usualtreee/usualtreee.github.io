二、Part 1: Walking

Q1: How much walking do you do in your daily life?

> “To be honest, I don’t walk much on weekdays because I usually ride an e-scooter to my lab, and since the campus is pretty small, there aren’t many chances to walk around. But on weekends, I love doing city walks in downtown Shanghai — places like Wukang Road or Huaihai Road. Shanghai is just such a comfortable city to explore on foot.”

Q2: Did you walk more when you were at school than now?

> “Yeah, I definitely walked more back then. I’m still a student now, but during my undergrad at Fuzhou University, the campus was huge and I lived on the sixth floor — and there was no elevator at all. So I had to climb up and down the stairs every single day. These days, my dorm has an elevator and I ride an e-scooter everywhere, so my walking has really dropped.”

Q3: What places are there to go for a walk near where you live?

> “Like I mentioned, I don’t really walk around campus much. I usually take the subway to the Bund or Lujiazui. I just love the vibe there — the mix of old and new architecture — and I actually enjoy how it’s always bustling with tourists. It feels really lively.”

Q4: Would you ever like to go on a walking holiday?

> “I’d love to, honestly. Right now my research and coursework are pretty intense, so it’s hard to find a big chunk of time. But I firmly believe that you should do the things you really want while you’re still young. So I’m thinking of squeezing in a short trip — maybe just a weekend or during a long break — rather than putting it off forever.”

三、Part 2: Describe a play or a film you’d like to see again with friends

Film: Avengers: Endgame

> “Well, the film I’d love to see again is Avengers: Endgame. I first watched it back in high school with a bunch of my classmates, and honestly, it was such a big deal for all of us at the time. > > To be honest, I’m not usually the kind of person who rewatches superhero movies, but this one is special because it basically opened the door to Marvel films for me. Before that, I hadn’t really been into Western superhero stuff, but Endgame completely changed my mind. > > I still remember my friends were absolutely obsessed with the characters — Iron Man, Captain America, all of them. They kept saying how exhilarating and action-packed it was, and that the final battle was just insanely satisfying. For them, it wasn’t just a movie; it was like the ultimate payoff for years of storytelling. > > But for me, the reason I want to see it again isn’t just about the film itself. It’s more about the feeling I had back then. I was young, carefree, and sitting in that cinema with my closest friends made me feel like the world was full of possibilities, you know? Everything felt larger-than-life, and I genuinely believed my future could be just as epic as the plot. > > So if I had the chance, I’d definitely go with those same high school friends. I guess part of me just wants to relive that moment and maybe recapture a bit of that optimism and excitement. Plus, now that we’re all busy with grad school and work, it’d be nice to just hang out like we used to, without worrying about deadlines or anything.”

四、Part 3: Theatres today / Actors

Q1: What are the most popular kinds of plays or shows at theatres in your country?

> “Well, I don’t think China has a particularly strong theatre culture, at least not in the way some European countries do. Traditional plays do exist, but they tend to attract a relatively small audience. In terms of what’s genuinely popular right now, I’d say stand-up comedy, live music, and e-sports tournaments are the dominant forms of live entertainment, especially among young people.”

Q2: How easy is it to get tickets to the theatre?

> “It really depends on how popular the show is. Buying tickets on your phone is super convenient these days — it’s just a few taps. But if it’s a really hot show, it can sell out in literally seconds.”

Q3: Do you think theatres need to do more to attract younger audiences?

> “Yeah, I think so. The thing is, traditional theatre performances often feel a bit disconnected from young people’s daily lives and culture. Since young people are basically the main consumer force now, theatres really need to adapt — whether it’s for keeping traditions alive or just staying in business. They should probably update their content or format to match what younger audiences actually enjoy.”

Q4: What do you think attracts people to working as an actor?

> “Well, I guess the money and fame definitely play a big part. But personally, I think what really draws people in is the chance to step into someone else’s shoes — to use your own emotions and understanding of life to bring a character to life. It’s a way to express your perspective to the audience, and at the same time, you get to experience a completely different life, which is pretty amazing. I think for creative people, expressing yourself is almost like a calling, and exploring different human experiences is what keeps them going.”

Q5: What qualities does a person need to become an actor? Any disadvantages?

> “Well, first of all, you definitely need to be resilient and willing to put in the hard work. Looks matter, but they’re not everything. What’s more important is having your own perspective on life — your own attitude and understanding of the world. Plus, you need to genuinely love performing and be able to convey emotions effectively. > > As for the downsides, I think actors sacrifice a huge chunk of their personal life. Their strengths and weaknesses are constantly under a microscope, and they can easily get swamped by public opinion — whether it’s praise or criticism.”
