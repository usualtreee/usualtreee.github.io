雅思口语语料库 — C20 Test 2 Speaking（Fruit / Changed a plan / Career）

一、Part 1: Fruit

Q1: What’s your favourite fruit?

> “Definitely mango. I just love the taste — it’s sweet but not overwhelming. Plus, I have this childhood memory of a mango tree right outside my house. Every summer it would produce these huge mangoes, and my mum would pick them for me. I get really nostalgic thinking about those days.”

Q2: Are there any kinds of fruit that you don’t like eating?

> “Honestly, I don’t really dislike any fruit. But if I had to pick one, I’d probably say apples. I mean, they’re super healthy, but they’re just so common that they feel a bit boring. Compared to mangoes or peaches, they’re not very juicy, and the flavor is a bit bland. Maybe that’s just my bias though.”

Q3: Do you like eating cooked food that has fruit in it?

> “Not really. I just find it off-putting — both the look and the taste. There’s something weird about hot, soggy fruit in a savoury dish; it gets all mushy and overly sweet. The only exception I’d make is pineapple — like in sweet-and-sour pork or on pizza. For some reason, that works for me.”

Q4: Where’s the best place to buy fruit where you live?

> “Honestly, the fruit shop on campus is kind of overpriced, so I usually avoid it. Instead, I buy from these small stalls right outside the school gate — they’re way cheaper. Or I go to Hema, which is like a fresh grocery store here. The quality there is definitely better, but the street stalls are unbeatable when it comes to price.”

二、Part 2: Describe a time when you changed a plan

But then I realized my girlfriend’s graduation ceremony was coming up really soon, and I was worried I might miss it if I went to Yinchuan first. 三、Part 3: Making plans with friends / Planning a career

Q1: What kinds of plans do friends make together?

> “I’d say travel plans are probably the most common ones. Like, my friends and I are always scheming short weekend trips — just hopping on a train to nearby cities, hitting up local restaurants, and checking out whatever sights are there. Sometimes it’s just a day trip to a nearby town for good food and a change of scenery.”

Q2: Do you think it’s better to discuss future plans with friends or with family?

> “I would argue that neither is superior; rather, they fulfil distinct functions. Family discussions tend to provide emotional scaffolding — unconditional support and encouragement — even if the advice lacks professional specificity. Conversely, peers can offer multifaceted perspectives rooted in shared generational experiences, and their support tends to be both empathetic and pragmatically effective because they face analogous challenges.”

Q3: When making plans for the future, is it important not to copy friends?

> “Yeah, absolutely. I don’t think we should blindly copy our friends’ paths. Everyone’s situation is different. We should really listen to our own instincts and stay committed to our personal goals, making plans based on our unique strengths and circumstances.”

Q4: When people are choosing what to study, how important is it that their course should lead directly to a career?

> “Well, I think it’s pretty important, especially nowadays. University education in China often lags behind the industry — it’s either too theory-heavy or purely research-oriented. For students who want to land a job straight after graduation, that’s a real disadvantage. If you want to map out a solid career path, you pretty much have to pick up practical, industry-relevant skills on your own. That said, the foundational knowledge and general education you get at university are still crucial — they’re basically the bedrock of whatever career you pursue.”

Q5: Why is it a good idea to get some work experience before deciding on a future career?

> “Well, like I just said, you need to get your hands dirty with industry stuff. An internship lets you dive straight into how a company actually works, so you pick up practical skills way faster and more accurately than in a classroom. There’s this Chinese saying — basically, book knowledge only gets you so far; to truly understand something, you gotta do it yourself. Work experience is exactly that.”

Q6: How easy do you think it is for people to change from one career to another?

> “I think it really depends on the role. If you’re aiming for something technical that requires specialized knowledge — like engineering or programming — then switching fields takes serious effort. But if it’s more repetitive work or service-oriented jobs, it’s probably much easier. It also depends on what you were doing before. Generally speaking, people from more demanding roles can transition to simpler ones more smoothly — it’s almost like natural downward compatibility.”
